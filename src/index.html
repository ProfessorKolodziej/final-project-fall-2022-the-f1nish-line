Welcome to the thrilling world of Formula 1 racing

Since its inaugural race in 1950, Formula 1 has become the pinnacle of international motorsport competition. But why the name "Formula 1"? Well, it's more than just a number – it represents a set of meticulously crafted rules and regulations that all participants must follow. The term "formula" embodies these guidelines, while the number 'one' stands as a symbol of the highest level of racing on the global stage. The Formula One World Championship takes us on an exhilarating journey from March to December, spanning 21 races in 21 different countries across four continents. Get ready to dive into the world of speed, technology, and the pursuit of excellence as we explore the captivating universe of Formula 1.

The Teams

There are ten teams that participate in the Formula 1 World Championship every year. Each team is called a ‘constructor’. Teams receive points based on their drivers’ performance in each race. These points determine the team’s ranking as they compete for the annual Constructors’ Championship within the Formula 1 World Championship.

The Drivers

Each team nominates two drivers who race for them throughout the annual championship. Drivers receive points based on their rankings in each race. The driver with the highest number of points at the end of all the races wins the Drivers’ Championship within the Formula 1 World Championship.

The Grand Prix

The heart of Formula 1 racing beats to the rhythm of Grand Prix weekends, a three-day spectacle from Friday to Sunday that ignites the passion of fans around the world. The racing events that occur at each Formula 1 track are collectively called a Grand Prix.

Free Practice

The Grand Prix journey kicks off on a Friday, featuring two adrenaline-fueled one-hour practice sessions known as Free Practice 1 (FP1) and Free Practice 2 (FP2). It's here that drivers get their first taste of the race track, honing their skills and fine-tuning their cars. As the day progresses, they push their machines to the limit, gearing up for the challenges ahead.

Sprint Race

Occasionally, the Grand Prix weekend takes an exhilarating twist with the inclusion of Sprint races. In 2023, we're in for a treat with six Sprint weekends. The Sprint is a high-speed 100km dash, setting the grid order for the main event on Sunday. Sprint weekends follow a unique format, featuring practice, qualifying, the Sprint race, and, as always, the Grand Prix on Sunday. It's a weekend filled with non-stop action that keeps fans at the edge of their seats.

Qualifying

Saturday afternoon ushers in the intense battle of Qualifying, divided into three stages: Q1, Q2, and Q3, with each stage getting shorter and more competitive. The stakes are high, as the fastest drivers secure their positions at the front, known as pole position. For those who don't make the cut, it's a tough break, and they'll start the race from further back on the grid. Qualifying is the stage where teams and drivers showcase their speed, precision, and determination to win.

The Race

Sunday is the day we've all been waiting for, as the Grand Prix takes center stage. Before the race begins, drivers embark on reconnaissance laps to assess track conditions and make final preparations. They then line up on the grid according to their qualifying positions, and after the national anthem, it's time for the formation lap. Watch as drivers weave their cars, warming up their tires in anticipation of the Grand Prix's green light. As the race director initiates the start sequence, the five red lights illuminate, extinguishing one by one before the roar of engines fills the air.

The race is on! Grands Prix typically last up to two hours or reach the minimum distance of laps that exceeds 305 kilometers. In 2023, points are awarded based on performance and the driver who crosses the finish line first wins the Grand Prix, with an extra point for the driver who records the fastest race lap within the top 10.

Tires

Understanding Tires in Formula 1 can get very overwhelming very fast however they are an intregal part of a team’s strategy throughout the weekend. Thus we hope to break it down a bit for everyone to understand this a bit better.

Weather-based Tires

To account for the weather variable Pirelli has created three different weather based tires. The first type and the most common one are the slick tires. These tires are the fastest thus often the tires drivers prefer. However they also have the least amount of grip thus not ideal for rainy weather. The following tires are the intermediates. These tires are used for exactly what their name tells them to be used for. They are ideal for light rain and are have an average amount of grip and speed. The last type of tires are wet tires which have the most grip but are the slowest. However sometimes they are needed to ensure the safety of the drivers.

Tire Compounds

In order to make the races more interesting Pirelli has 6 different compounds of tires for teams to choose between. Giving teams these options really adds pressure to them in ensuring they apply the best strategy throughout the weekend. Pirelli has divided the different compounds into three main categories soft, medium and hard. The soft tires are sleekest tires so they go the fastest but they also last the least amount of time which can mean the team has to add an extra pit stop causing their driver to loose time. The hard tires have the most rigids causing them to slow down but they also last longer often making them ideal for tracks with extremely high temperatures that can increase tire degradation. Lastly there are medium tires which again their name is very indicative as to what they do. These tires right in between in terms of duration and speed often being the main choice for drivers and teams.

DRS

DRS is a racing component that has been introduced with modern racing. It was introduced as a way to help drivers race with each other and increase the amount of overtaking within each race.

DRS or drag reduction system allows drivers open a flap within their rear wing to reduce the amount of drag within their car to increase their car’s speed and hopefully overtake the car in front of them. However this system can only be activated within specific sections of a track and only if the distance between their car and the car in front of them is one second or less.
